\section*{Abstract}

Policy microsimulation models rely on imputation to attach variables one
survey observes to records from another, and the imputed file is then
reweighted --- calibrated to administrative totals, filtered to
subpopulations, stressed under reform scenarios. Common imputation practice
fits models to survey records while ignoring their design weights, which
biases imputed conditionals toward the sample rather than the population and
plants extreme low-weight donor values that a later reweighting can amplify.
We present the imputation operator of \populace{}, \policyengine{}'s
open-source microdata stack: a regime-gated, sequentially-chained,
weighted-bootstrap quantile-regression-forest estimator whose interface makes
an unweighted fit impossible to request by accident. We benchmark it against
standard survey-imputation methods --- unweighted and weighted quantile
regression forests, ordinary least squares, linear quantile regression, and
nearest-neighbour hot-deck statistical matching --- on \todo{task count} tasks
including \scf{}-to-\cps{} wealth imputation and zero-inflated tax-record
components, under a paired, repeated holdout protocol scored with weighted
distributional metrics. Ablations attribute performance to each design choice:
the weighted bootstrap \todo{weighted-vs-unweighted result}, regime gating
\todo{gating result}, and sequential chaining \todo{chaining result}. A
reweighting stress test shows \todo{fragility result}, and an application to
the Supplemental Security Income savings penalty illustrates the policy
stakes: \todo{SSI application result}. The estimator is available as
\populacefit{}, installable independently of the rest of the stack.
